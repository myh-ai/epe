Escaping Planet Earth is not a flight from reality. It is a record of the moments when reality becomes too narrow for the questions it provokes.

This is a personal notebook left open; a quiet room for stepping away from inherited thoughts and following the extraordinary hiding just beyond the ordinary. The essays here drift across the fault lines of cinema and memory, love and loss, artificial minds and human souls, old manuscripts and future anxieties. A piece might begin with a song echoing in a dark theater. Another with a blue folder from a life that feels almost borrowed. Sometimes with a machine on a psychiatrist's couch, performing a sorrow it has never had to survive.

What binds this space is not a single subject, but a recurring obsession: the human being, and the many ways we are expressed, measured, misunderstood, priced, governed, loved, lost, and remembered.

The disciplines I keep returning to only look separate from a distance. Language asks how we speak ourselves into the world. Literature asks what remains in the silence after we speak. History asks what the past continues to build — or break — inside us. Finance and economics ask how labor, time, risk, and desire become value, debt, price, and policy. Artificial intelligence asks what happens when our words, our pain, our choices, and our patterns are modeled by machines, often with terrifying fluency, and without ever having lived a human life.

At the core of this blog is a recurring question: what happens to the human soul when it is translated into a sentence or a story; a transaction or a dataset; a parameter or a prediction?

I write fiction and essays, and I work between artificial intelligence research and financial practice. From the outside, these might seem like entirely separate lives. To me, they are different coordinates on the same map. Finance keeps me close to the friction of the actual world: labor, value, risk, institutions, and the quiet violence of being counted. Writing and research let me rise just far enough above that machinery to see its shape, not to escape it, but to ask what it does to us.

From there, the pattern begins to show itself. The human being is pressured twice over: first by the material systems that price, rank, discipline, and exhaust us; then by the symbolic systems that name us, narrate us, classify us, and increasingly model us. A salary, a debt, a file, a diagnosis, a story, a dataset, a prediction, each claim to hold some truth about a life, and each inevitably leaves something behind.

Ultimately, both writing and research are attempts to trace what remains. They are efforts to decipher how we are represented, what is done to us through that representation, and what still escapes every system that tries to hold us completely.

This is why the writings here resist the flattening of human experience. They refuse to let grief, dignity, nostalgia, longing, and wonder become merely data points, especially in a world growing more fluent at treating them that way.
